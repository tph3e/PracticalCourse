\subsubsection{Reinforcement-Learning Policy}\label{ssub:rl-eval}
\textit{(Aldo Patrone)}\quad
The RL policy (Sec.~\ref{subsec:rl}) is evaluated at two levels. A fast controlled
case-based environment, calibrated to BPIC-17 (arrival rate and case length matched),
serves method development and is illustrated in the Appendix
(Fig.~\ref{fig:alloc_cmp_plot}): the learned agents trade cycle time against fairness,
yet the simple \emph{shortest-queue} heuristic already matches them, since greedy
load-balancing serves fairness directly. The robust verdict comes from the integrated
simulator, where every method runs through the identical event engine and is scored by
\emph{compute\_all} (Sec.~\ref{ssub:metrics}) over five random seeds under a congested
load (Table~\ref{tab:alloc_sim}). No deep-learning variant beats shortest-queue: the
fairness Gini is a statistical tie across all methods (about $0.58 \pm 0.02$), while
shortest-queue clearly leads resource occupation ($0.77$ against roughly $0.54$ to
$0.65$). Average cycle time was $0.3$ days for every method, so allocation barely moves
it and it is left out of the table.

This negative result is deliberately robust rather than an artifact of weak training.
The obvious counter-explanations were each closed: the training and inference load
features were aligned to the same quantity, the fairness signal was made scale-invariant
so it does not vanish over long episodes, the policy was trained directly on the
integrated simulator (sim-in-the-loop), the state-of-the-art \emph{MaskablePPO} was
included and a PPO learner was trained on the simulator itself, warm-started from the
surrogate policy, as in Middelhuis et al.~\cite{middelhuis2025rl}. None surpasses the
heuristic. The explanation is structural: shortest-queue is near-optimal for the
load-Gini fairness metric, while cycle time is insensitive to allocation in this process,
so the added complexity of a learned policy is not justified for this objective. The
full cross-method comparison of all group methods is synthesized in
Sec.~\ref{ssub:cross-eval}.
